1. The Phenomenon (Physics)

An Urban Heat Island (UHI) occurs when a city experiences significantly warmer temperatures than its surrounding rural areas. This study investigates Kakkanad through the lens of thermal physics.

The Physics Drivers

  • Albedo: Urban surfaces (asphalt, concrete) absorb sunlight, whereas rural vegetation reflects it.
  • Thermal Mass: Concrete acts as a "thermal sponge," releasing heat slowly at night.
  • Evapotranspiration: The loss of trees removes the natural cooling effect of water vapor release.

Governing Equation: Stefan-Boltzmann Law

We bridge the gap between satellite-measured energy and actual temperature using this law:

$$ E = \sigma \cdot \varepsilon \cdot T^4 $$
  • \( E \) : Radiant Energy measured by satellite
  • \( \sigma \) : Stefan-Boltzmann constant
  • \( \varepsilon \) : Emissivity (Efficiency of radiating heat)
  • \( T \) : Land Surface Temperature (LST)

3. LST Algorithm Implementation

The core methodology for retrieving Land Surface Temperature involves four distinct computational steps.

Step A: Cloud Masking

Clouds distort thermal readings. We use the QA_PIXEL band to filter out cloud and shadow pixels.

function maskLandsat(image){
  var qa = image.select('QA_PIXEL');
  // Mask clouds and cloud shadows using bitwise logic
  var mask = qa.bitwiseAnd(1<<3).eq(0)
              .and(qa.bitwiseAnd(1<<5).eq(0));
  return image.updateMask(mask);
}

Step B: NDVI Calculation

We need vegetation data to estimate surface emissivity.

$$ NDVI = \frac{NIR - Red}{NIR + Red} $$
var ndvi = image.normalizedDifference(['SR_B5','SR_B4']).rename('NDVI');

Step C: Emissivity Correction (\(\varepsilon\))

Different surfaces radiate heat differently. We derive Emissivity using Proportion of Vegetation (\(P_v\)).

$$ P_v = \left( \frac{NDVI - NDVI_{min}}{NDVI_{max} - NDVI_{min}} \right)^2 $$
$$ \varepsilon = 0.004 \cdot P_v + 0.986 $$
var pv = ndvi.subtract(min).divide(max.subtract(min)).pow(2);
var emissivity = pv.multiply(0.004).add(0.986);

Step D: Single-Channel Algorithm (LST)

Finally, we convert Brightness Temperature (BT) to LST.

$$ LST = \frac{BT}{1 + (\lambda \cdot \frac{BT}{14380}) \cdot \ln(\varepsilon)} - 273.15 $$
var lst = thermal.expression(
 '(BT / (1 + (0.00115 * BT / 14380) * log(eps))) - 273.15', {
  'BT': thermal,
  'eps': emissivity
});

4. The Suspects: Spectral Analysis

To understand the root cause of the heat, we move beyond visual maps to statistical correlation. We analyze two key spectral indices.

NDVI (Vegetation Health)

The Theory: Healthy vegetation absorbs Red light (for photosynthesis) and strongly reflects Near-Infrared (NIR) light. We use this contrast to measure plant density.

$$ NDVI = \frac{NIR - Red}{NIR + Red} $$
  • NIR: Near-Infrared (High reflectance = Healthy)
  • Red: Visible Red (High absorption = Healthy)
// Landsat 8 Calculation
var ndvi = image.normalizedDifference(['SR_B5','SR_B4']);

Figure 4a: Scatter plot of Vegetation vs. Temperature

The Verdict: The chart above reveals a distinct Negative Correlation. As NDVI increases (moving right), surface temperature drops significantly (by 4-5°C). This mathematically proves that vegetation is the primary cooling sink in Kakkanad.

NDBI (Built-Up Density)

The Theory: Urban materials like asphalt and concrete have a unique spectral signature—they reflect Short-Wave Infrared (SWIR) light more intensely than Near-Infrared (NIR).

$$ NDBI = \frac{SWIR - NIR}{SWIR + NIR} $$
  • SWIR: Short-Wave Infrared (Reflected by concrete)
  • NIR: Near-Infrared (Reflected by trees)
// Landsat 8 Calculation
var ndbi = image.normalizedDifference(['SR_B6','SR_B5']);

Figure 4b: Scatter plot of Concrete vs. Temperature

The Verdict: The chart confirms a strong Positive Correlation. As concrete density rises (moving right), temperature spikes. This identifies impervious surfaces as the "Heating Element" driving the UHI effect.

5. Integration with QGIS

The Theory (Interoperability): While Google Earth Engine handles the heavy computation (Petabyte-scale processing), desktop GIS (QGIS) is required for precise cartographic layout and vector-raster overlay. This step demonstrates the transition from cloud computing to local analysis.

We use the Export module to convert the raw pixel data into a GeoTIFF format.

// Exporting the computed LST matrix to Drive
Export.image.toDrive({
  image: lst,
  description: 'Kakkanad_LST_Analysis',
  scale: 30, // 30m Native Resolution
  region: roi,
  fileFormat: 'GeoTIFF'
});

Figure 5: The LST Data successfully imported into QGIS 3.28 (Firenze)

The Verdict: Successful export confirms that our LST data is spatially referenced (EPSG:4326) and pixel-aligned with local ward boundaries. This enables further "Hotspot Analysis" which cannot be easily performed in a web browser.

8. Strategic Interventions

Based on the hotspot analysis (Figure 4) and spectral correlation data (Figure 5), we propose a targeted "Blue-Green-Grey" retrofit strategy for Kakkanad.

The Green Grid

Target: Zones with NDBI > 0.1
Action: Mandate "Green Corridors" along the Seaport-Airport Road. Our NDVI data proves that a 20% increase in canopy cover correlates to a 3°C drop in LST.

The Blue Buffer

Target: Low Elevation (< 12m) Zones
Action: Strictly preserve the Edachira wetlands. These act as natural "evaporative coolers," mitigating the heat radiating from Infopark's concrete mass.

Cool Albedo Retrofit

Target: Industrial Hotspots (> 37°C)
Action: Implement High-Albedo (White) Roofs on all industrial warehouses. This simple intervention reflects 80% of solar incidence, directly reducing the "Furnace Effect."
